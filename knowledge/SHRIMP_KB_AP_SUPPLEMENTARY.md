## WHY PL QUALITY = 50% OF CROP SUCCESS

"You can have the best pond, best feed, best water quality — but if your PL is weak, the crop will fail."
— AP aquaculture officer saying

## PL SELECTION CRITERIA — AP FARMER CHECKLIST

### At the Hatchery — Before Purchase
| Check | What to Look For | Reject If |
|---|---|---|
| PCR status | Request PCR certificate: WSSV, EHP, AHPND, IHHNV negative | Any positive result |
| Hatchery certification | BAP, MPEDA, or state aquaculture certification | Uncertified; unknown source |
| Broodstock origin | SPF (Specific Pathogen Free) certified broodstock | Wild-caught broodstock |
| Hatchery biosecurity | Separate tanks per batch; foot baths; no visitor access | Poor biosecurity visible |
| Water quality records | Should maintain records of salinity, pH, DO during rearing | No records available |
| PL age | PL10–PL15 for vannamei (most common) | Too young (<PL8) or too old (>PL20) |

### PL Visual Quality Check
| Parameter | Healthy PL | Reject If |
|---|---|---|
| Swimming behavior | Active, strong against current; swim upward | Weak; settle at bottom; no current response |
| Body color | Transparent with dark chromatophores visible | Pale white; yellow; milky appearance |
| Gut | Full gut visible (dark line) | Empty gut = not feeding = stressed |
| Body shape | Straight body; full tail fan | Bent body; cramped muscle; deformed rostrum |
| Size uniformity | >80% uniform size | High variation = poor hatchery management |
| Muscle | Clear muscles with normal color | White/opaque muscle = IHHNV or stress |
| Hepatopancreas | Orange-yellow; full and plump | Pale/shrunken HP = disease or stress |
| Activity when disturbed | Jump actively; quick response | Sluggish; no response = weak PL |

### PL Stress Test (Formaldehyde Stress Test — FST)
**Purpose:** Test PL quality and stress tolerance before purchase
**Procedure:**
1. Take 10 PLs from the batch
2. Place in 50% freshwater (dilute pond water with equal fresh water) for 30 minutes
3. Count survivors at 30 minutes

| Survivors | PL Quality | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 9–10/10 survive | EXCELLENT | Safe to stock |
| 7–8/10 survive | GOOD | Acceptable |
| 5–6/10 survive | MODERATE | Negotiate; consider rejection |
| <5/10 survive | POOR | REJECT ENTIRE BATCH |

### Salinity Acclimatization Before Stocking
- Hatchery salinity may differ from pond salinity by 5–15 ppt
- Never introduce PLs directly if salinity difference >5 ppt
- **Gradual acclimatization protocol:**
  - Float oxygen bags in pond: 20–30 minutes (temperature matching)
  - Add 10–20% pond water to bag: 5 minutes
  - Add another 10–20% pond water: 5 minutes
  - Add another 10–20% pond water: 5 minutes
  - Then release PLs — total time: 40–50 minutes
- Stock during coolest part of day: 4–6 AM or after 6 PM
- Add Vitamin C to release area: 250g/acre

# SECTION 12: BIOFLOC TECHNOLOGY (BFT) FOR SHRIMP
# ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

## WHAT IS BIOFLOC?

Biofloc Technology (BFT) is an intensive shrimp farming system where:
- NO water exchange — zero or minimal
- Beneficial microorganisms (bacteria + algae + protozoa) form "floc" particles
- Floc serves as supplemental protein feed for shrimp (10–20% of nutritional needs)
- Ammonia is converted to microbial protein IN the pond
- Possible stocking density: 100–300 PL/m² (much higher than conventional)
- AP adoption: Growing rapidly in Nellore, Godavari belt, indoor tank systems

## BFT WATER PARAMETERS — DIFFERENT FROM CONVENTIONAL

| Parameter | Conventional Pond | Biofloc System | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| DO | >5 mg/L | >6 mg/L (higher demand) | Aerate aggressively 24/7 |
| pH | 7.8–8.3 | 7.0–8.0 | Slightly lower optimal; monitor closely |
| Salinity | 10–25 ppt | 10–25 ppt | Same |
| TAN (Ammonia) | <1 mg/L | <2 mg/L | Biofloc can handle slightly higher |
| Nitrite | <0.1 mg/L | <0.5 mg/L | Floc helps convert faster |
| Nitrate | <50 mg/L | Up to 200 mg/L acceptable | Higher nitrate is OK in BFT |
| TSS (Total Suspended Solids) | 20–50 mg/L | 200–600 mg/L | Floc particles ARE the suspended solids |
| Alkalinity | 100–150 mg/L | 150–200 mg/L (HIGHER NEED) | Nitrification consumes alkalinity |
| Floc volume (Imhoff cone) | — | 5–15 mL/L optimal | Measure every morning |
| Secchi depth | 30–45 cm | 20–30 cm (turbid from floc) | Different from clear pond standard |
| Temperature | 23–30°C | 23–30°C | Same |

## BFT CARBON:NITROGEN (C:N) RATIO — KEY CONCEPT
- Adding carbon source keeps C:N ratio >15:1 → bacteria thrive → consume ammonia
- AP carbon sources used: Molasses (most common), Tapioca flour, Rice bran, Wheat flour

| Carbon Source | C:N Ratio | Application Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Molasses | ~65:1 | 50–200 g/m³ when TAN >1 mg/L | Most common in AP; cheap |
| Tapioca/cassava starch | ~50:1 | 50–150 g/m³ | Effective; clean |
| Rice bran | ~35:1 | 100–300 g/m³ | Widely available in AP |
| Wheat flour | ~40:1 | 100–200 g/m³ | Good but expensive |
| Sago | ~60:1 | 50–150 g/m³ | Very effective |

**How to use:** When TAN goes above 1 mg/L → Add carbon source at 10–15× the TAN increase in grams/m³

## BFT FLOC MANAGEMENT

### Measuring Floc Volume (Imhoff Cone)
1. Fill 1-liter Imhoff cone with pond water
2. Let settle for 15 minutes
3. Read floc volume at bottom

| Floc Volume | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| <5 mL/L | Too little floc | Add probiotic + carbon source; reduce water exchange |
| 5–15 mL/L | OPTIMAL | Maintain current management |
| 15–30 mL/L | Getting dense | Reduce carbon addition; partial water removal |
| >30 mL/L | Dangerous overload | Remove 20–30% floc; reduce feeding; risk of DO crash |

### BFT Probiotic Application
- Add Bacillus-based probiotics at start to establish healthy microbial community
- Growel Pond Pro: 2 kg/acre at start; 1 kg/acre weekly after
- Do NOT use disinfectants (BKC, chlorine) in biofloc ponds — kills your floc!

### BFT Alkalinity Management
- Nitrification consumes 7.1 g alkalinity per gram of ammonia oxidized
- Must supplement alkalinity regularly in BFT
- **Alkalinity supplement:** Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda): 5–10 kg/acre when alkalinity <100 mg/L
- Check alkalinity daily in BFT — far more critical than in conventional ponds

### BFT vs Conventional — AP Comparison

| Feature | Conventional | Biofloc |
|---|---|---|
| Water exchange | 20–30%/week | Zero or minimal |
| Stocking density | 30–60 PL/m² | 100–300 PL/m² |
| Yield | 8–15 ton/acre | 20–50 ton/acre |
| Capital cost | Low | HIGH (tanks, aeration, infrastructure) |
| Operating cost | Medium | Medium-High (carbon source, probiotics) |
| Disease risk | Moderate | Lower (closed system) |
| WSSV risk | Moderate | Very Low (no water exchange) |
| Water consumption | High | Very Low — eco-friendly |
| Skill required | Moderate | HIGH — daily monitoring essential |
| Best for AP zones | All zones | Indoor/tank systems; Nellore industrial farms |

## UNDERSTANDING THE MOLT CYCLE

Molting (shedding of shell) is the MOST VULNERABLE time for shrimp:
- Shrimp is completely soft for 6–12 hours after molting
- Cannot defend itself — extremely susceptible to cannibalism
- Cannot escape predators or other shrimp
- Needs urgent calcium and magnesium to harden new shell

### Molting Frequency by Size
| Shrimp Size | Molting Frequency | What This Means |
|---|---|---|
| PL stage | Every 1–3 days | Very rapid; critical mineral supply |
| 1–5g | Every 5–7 days | Weekly mineral pulses needed |
| 5–15g | Every 7–10 days | Bi-weekly mineral application |
| 15–25g | Every 10–14 days | Fortnightly mineral application |
| >25g (monodon) | Every 14–21 days | Every 2–3 weeks |

### Molting Signs in Pond
| Sign | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Empty shells in feed tray | Molting occurring — REDUCE FEED 30% |
| Increased cannibalism | Many shrimp soft shell simultaneously |
| Shrimp near edges | Seeking shelter during soft shell period |
| Feed tray residue increases | Soft shrimp don't eat; others attacking them |
| Temporary feeding reduction | Normal — resume full feeding after 24h |

### Molting Management Protocol
| Timing | Action | Product | Dose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2–3 days BEFORE molting event | Pre-load minerals | Growel Aqua Mineral | 5 kg/acre + 10 g/kg feed |
| During molting (shells in tray) | Reduce feeding 30–50% | — | — |
| During molting | Apply dolomite | Dolomite | 50 kg/acre |
| IMMEDIATELY after molting | Mineral boost | Growel Aqua Mineral + Intas AquaMins | 5 kg/acre each |
| 12–24h after molting | Resume normal feeding | — | — |
| After molting in low-salinity ponds | Potassium supplement | Potassium chloride (KCl) | 5–10 kg/acre |

### Synchronizing Molting (Advanced Management)
- Synchronous molting = all shrimp molt together = HIGHER RISK (mass cannibalism)
- Asynchronous (staggered) molting = safer = less cannibalism at any one time
- To promote asynchronous molting: vary feeding rate; maintain good water quality variation

### Mineral Deficiency Signs During Molting
| Sign | Deficiency | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Shell remains soft >12h | Ca or Mg deficiency | Dolomite + chelated minerals urgently |
| Cannibalism spike | Soft shell too long | Minerals + reduce density |
| Shell pits/deformity | Multiple mineral deficiency | Broad-spectrum mineral mix |
| Black shell lesions post-molt | Bacterial invasion of new shell | Probiotics + KMnO₄ bath |
| Poor color after molt | General nutritional issue | Vitamin C + mineral + feed quality check |

# SECTION 14: POND DESIGN & INFRASTRUCTURE — AP SHRIMP
# ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

## STANDARD AP SHRIMP POND SPECIFICATIONS

### Pond Size and Shape
| Parameter | Recommended | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pond size | 0.5–2.0 hectares (1.2–5 acres) | Too large = poor management; too small = poor economics |
| Optimal size AP | 1–1.5 ha (2.5–4 acres) | Most common in AP coastal zones |
| Shape | Rectangular or square | Oval best for aeration efficiency |
| Depth | 1.2–1.5 m (water depth) | <1.0 m = temperature fluctuation; >1.5 m = poor light penetration |
| Length:width ratio | 2:1 to 3:1 | Allows better paddlewheel positioning |
| Bund (embankment) height | 1.5–2.0 m above water | Must be above highest flood level |
| Bund width (top) | 3–5 m | Allow vehicle access; maintenance |
| Bund slope | 1:1.5 (inner); 1:2 (outer) | Prevent erosion |

### Inlet and Outlet Design
| Component | Specification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Inlet pipe/sluice | PVC or concrete; 20–30 cm diameter | At highest point of pond; allows gravity fill |
| Inlet screen | 300 micron mesh | Exclude wild organisms |
| Outlet (monk drain) | At deepest point of pond | Allows complete drainage |
| Outlet screen | 2mm mesh initially → finer as PLs grow | Prevent escape |
| Overflow pipe | 10 cm above normal water level | Safety valve for heavy rain |
| Central drain | Preferred for intensive systems | Removes sludge from center |
| Bottom slope | 0.5–1% toward outlet | Complete drainage capability |

### Aeration System Design
| Culture System | Paddlewheel HP/acre | Placement | Operating Hours |
|---|---|---|---|
| Semi-intensive (20–30 PL/m²) | 3–5 HP | 2–4 units arranged for circular flow | Night + dawn (minimum) |
| Intensive (30–60 PL/m²) | 6–10 HP | 4–8 units; circular flow pattern | 24/7 |
| Super-intensive (60–100 PL/m²) | 10–15 HP | 6–10 units + bottom diffusers | 24/7; never stop |

**Aeration layout principle:** Paddlewheels should create circular water flow → pushes sediment to center → allows edge access for shrimp → concentrates organic waste for removal

### Lining Options
| Lining Type | Cost | Durability | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Earthen (no lining) | Lowest | Variable (soil dependent) | Most common AP semi-intensive ponds |
| HDPE liner (High Density Polyethylene) | High | 10–15 years | Best for intensive + super-intensive |
| Concrete (partial or full) | Highest | 20+ years | Industrial farms; hatcheries |
| Bentonite clay lining | Medium | 5–8 years | Seals leaky ponds |
| Geomembrane LDPE | Medium-high | 8–12 years | Good middle option |

**AP Intensive Farm Trend:** HDPE-lined ponds with central drain → enables easy sludge removal → reduces H₂S risk dramatically

### Infrastructure Checklist — AP Shrimp Farm
| Infrastructure | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Reservoir pond (10–20% of farm area) | Water treatment + storage | Chlorinate incoming water here first |
| Feed storage room | Keep feed dry and cool | Raised floor; ventilated; pest-proof |
| Aeration machinery room | Generator + paddlewheel control | Must have backup generator |
| Weighing and sampling area | ABW measurement; feed calculation | Shade cover; clean surface |
| Guard/watchman house | 24/7 monitoring | Night monitoring critical |
| Bird exclusion nets | WSSV prevention | Over every production pond |
| Perimeter bund fencing | Crab/predator exclusion | Especially important near mangroves |
| Water quality lab area | Basic testing | DO meter, pH meter, refractometer, test kits |
| Generator (backup power) | Aerator backup | Non-negotiable for intensive farms |

# SECTION 15: WATER EXCHANGE MANAGEMENT
# ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

## WHEN TO DO WATER EXCHANGE

| Trigger | Exchange Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Routine (semi-intensive) | 10–15% per week | Maintain freshness |
| Routine (intensive) | 20–30% per week | High density = high waste |
| High ammonia TAN >2 mg/L | 20–30% single exchange | After zeolite application |
| High nitrite >0.5 mg/L | 20–30% | Dilution fastest fix |
| Dense algae bloom (Secchi <25cm) | 20–30% | Prevent bloom crash |
| After disease event | 30–40% | Dilute pathogen load |
| After heavy rain (salinity dilution) | NONE or add seawater | Salinity already dropped; don't dilute further |
| Pre-harvest | None needed | Stop 2 days before harvest |

## WHEN NOT TO DO WATER EXCHANGE

| Situation | Why to Avoid | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| During WSSV outbreak | Risk spreading virus to environment | Biosecurity containment instead |
| When intake water quality is poor | Bad water in = worse pond | Check source first; treat if necessary |
| When inlet canal has disease | Source water contaminated | Delay; use internal management |
| During heavy rain (salinity drop ongoing) | Will drop salinity further | Wait for rain to stop; add minerals |
| When pond algae is healthy and stable | Disrupts established bloom | Only exchange if parameter problem |
| Biofloc system (routine) | Destroys floc community | Use carbon management instead |

## WATER EXCHANGE PROTOCOL — AP SHRIMP

1. **Always screen inlet water:** 300 micron mesh → prevent wild organisms
2. **Check inlet water quality first:** DO, pH, salinity must be acceptable
3. **Timing:** Exchange in early morning when pond DO is naturally lowest → fresh water adds oxygen
4. **Exchange rate:** Max 30% per day — more than this = stress to shrimp from sudden change
5. **After rain:** Check salinity of inlet before allowing water in — may be too fresh

## TIDAL WATER MANAGEMENT (AP COASTAL FARMS)

AP coastal farms often use tidal water exchange:

| Tidal State | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| High tide (incoming) | Open inlet — bring fresh seawater | Better quality; higher DO; higher salinity |
| Low tide | Close inlet | Outflow water may be contaminated with runoff |
| Pre-monsoon season | Extra screening + chlorination | Runoff contamination risk |
| Post-cyclone | Close all inlets; assess water quality | Contamination risk after storm surge |

## PLANKTON — WHY IT MATTERS FOR SHRIMP

Good plankton bloom in shrimp ponds:
- Produces oxygen (DO) during daytime through photosynthesis
- Provides natural food for PLs and juveniles (10–30% of nutritional needs)
- Stabilizes pH (buffers against swings)
- Reduces water clarity (prevents bottom algae + UV penetration)
- Competes with harmful algae species

## IDEAL PLANKTON FOR AP SHRIMP PONDS

| Plankton Type | Color | Benefit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diatoms (Chaetoceros, Skeletonema) | Brown-green | BEST — high nutrition; stable; good DO | Ideal water color for shrimp |
| Chlorella spp. | Bright green | Good; stable; produces DO | Common AP pond bloom |
| Nannochloropsis | Light green | Excellent nutritional value | Used in hatcheries + nursery |
| Tetraselmis | Yellow-green | Good; nutritious | |
| Spirulina | Dark blue-green | Nutritious BUT can be cyanobacteria risk | Check species |

## HARMFUL PLANKTON — RECOGNIZE AND ACT

| Species | Color | Toxin | Symptoms in Shrimp | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Microcystis aeruginosa | Blue-green surface scum | Microcystin (liver toxin) | HP damage; feeding stops; deaths | 30% water exchange; copper sulfate 0.3 ppm |
| Anabaena spp. | Blue-green filaments | Neurotoxin | Erratic swimming; mass mortality | Emergency water exchange |
| Oscillatoria spp. | Dark blue-green; oily sheen | Anatoxin | Rapid deaths | Emergency management |
| Ceratium (Dinoflagellate) | Red-brown patches | Paralytic toxins | Mass mortality; DO crash | Emergency water exchange + chlorination |
| Noctiluca (Red tide) | Pink-red glow at night | Ammonia release | Mass mortality | Emergency action |

## POST-HARVEST QUALITY MANAGEMENT

### Harvest Best Practices — AP Shrimp
| Step | Action | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Stop feeding | 24 hours before harvest | Clean gut; better meat quality |
| Harvest time | 4–6 AM (night harvest) | Cool temperature; shrimp active; less stress |
| Equipment | Clean nets; ice boxes ready; clean water for washing | Contamination prevention |
| Icing | Immediately ice shrimp after catching | 1:1 ice:shrimp ratio; target core temp <4°C in 15 min |
| De-heading | For processing — remove head at collection point | Slows deterioration; removes black gill (if present) |
| Grading | Sort by count: 40/50, 50/60, 60/70, 70/80, 80/100 | Price per kg varies by count |
| Transport | Ice boxes; insulated vehicles; minimize time | Reach processing plant within 4–6 hours maximum |

### Shrimp Count Grades — AP Market
| Count (per kg) | Size Name | Market | Price Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| U/10, U/15 | Jumbos | Premium export; 5-star hotels | Highest |
| 16/20, 21/25 | Extra Large (XL) | Export; premium domestic | Very high |
| 26/30, 31/40 | Large (L) | Export + domestic | High |
| 41/50, 51/60 | Medium (M) | Domestic + some export | Medium |
| 61/70, 71/80 | Small (S) | Domestic market | Lower |
| 81/100, 100+ | Extra Small (XS) | Local; processing | Lowest |

**AP farm target:** 40/50 to 60/70 count for maximum market return (100–120 day crop)

### Black Spot Prevention (Melanosis) — Quality Issue
Black spots appear on shrimp shell after harvest due to enzyme activity:
- Reduces market price; rejected by export plants
- **Prevention:** Metabisulfite (sodium metabisulfite) dip at collection point
  - Dose: 1.5–2.0% solution (15–20g per liter water)
  - Dip immediately after icing: 1–2 minutes
  - Rinse with clean water
  - Reduces melanosis; extends shelf life
- **Alternative:** Speed — get shrimp iced and to plant faster

### Antibiotic Residue — Export Compliance
| Antibiotic | MRL (EU/USA) | Withdrawal Period | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chloramphenicol | ZERO TOLERANCE | BANNED — never use | Immediate rejection; criminal charge |
| Nitrofurans | ZERO TOLERANCE | BANNED — never use | Immediate rejection |
| Oxytetracycline | 100 ppb | 21 days | Harvest after withdrawal complete |
| Florfenicol | 100 ppb (US); 200 ppb (EU) | 15–30 days | Safe if withdrawn |
| Enrofloxacin | Not approved | Do not use in food fish/shrimp | Reject in EU/US |
| Erythromycin | Not approved for shrimp in many markets | Avoid | Risk |

**AP export farms:** MPEDA regularly tests randomly sampled shrimp from AP farms. One positive result = farm blacklisted from export.

| FCR | Feed Used | Feed Cost (@₹70/kg) | Cost Difference vs FCR 1.4 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.2 | 960 kg | ₹67,200 | Save ₹11,200 |
| 1.4 (baseline) | 1,120 kg | ₹78,400 | Baseline |
| 1.6 | 1,280 kg | ₹89,600 | Extra ₹11,200 |
| 1.8 | 1,440 kg | ₹1,00,800 | Extra ₹22,400 |
| 2.0 | 1,600 kg | ₹1,12,000 | Extra ₹33,600 |

**KEY INSIGHT:** FCR improvement of just 0.2 units = ₹11,000–12,000 savings per acre. This is why Kemin KEMZYME (₹2,500/kg; 500g/MT feed = ₹1,250 cost) makes economic sense.

### Break-Even Analysis
- **Break-even yield** at ₹350/kg and ₹3.0 lakh cost = **857 kg/acre**
- **Break-even price** at 800 kg yield and ₹3.0 lakh cost = **₹375/kg**
- AP average shrimp price range: ₹280–550/kg depending on season and size

### Key Profitability Drivers — AP Farmer
1. **Survival rate** — each 10% improvement = ~15% yield improvement
2. **FCR** — each 0.1 unit improvement = 5–8% cost reduction
3. **Market timing** — price varies ₹100–200/kg seasonally in AP
4. **Disease prevention** — one WSSV loss = 5–10 profitable crops to recover
5. **Correct stocking density** — over-stocking = poor FCR + disease risk

### TCBS Agar — Farm-Level Vibrio Monitoring
**Purpose:** Detect Vibrio bacteria in pond water before they cause disease
**How to use:**
1. Collect 1 mL of pond water (from 30–50 cm depth; near aerator)
2. Dilute in 9 mL sterile water (1:10 dilution)
3. Streak on TCBS agar plate with sterile loop
4. Incubate at 35°C for 24 hours
5. Count and read:

| Colony Color | Bacteria | Risk Level | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yellow colonies | V. cholerae, V. alginolyticus | Moderate | Monitor; probiotics |
| Green colonies | V. parahaemolyticus, V. harveyi | HIGH | Treat immediately |
| Total Vibrio <10²/mL | Low | Green light | Routine management |
| Total Vibrio 10²–10³/mL | Moderate | Caution | Increase probiotics; reduce feed |
| Total Vibrio >10³/mL | DANGEROUS | Red alert | BKC + probiotics; antibiotic if systemic |

## WHY NURSERY PHASE?

Traditional farming: Stock PLs directly into grow-out pond
Nursery farming: Raise PLs in small nursery tank/pond for 20–30 days → then transfer to grow-out pond

**Nursery advantages:**
- Higher survival in first 30 days (most critical period for EMS)
- Smaller pond = easier water quality control
- Ability to grade and cull weak shrimp before grow-out
- Allows cleaning and preparation of grow-out pond while nursery runs
- 2–3 crops per year possible with nursery system (vs 2 without)

|---|---|

| Salinity | Match hatchery salinity; acclimatize gradually |
| Aeration | Continuous; air stone per 100L |

## SHRIMP-FISH INTEGRATED FARMING (AP PRACTICE)

Some AP farmers integrate fish with shrimp to:
- Utilize pond resources more efficiently
- Provide biological control of algae
- Additional income from fish
- Better water quality (fish consume organic matter)

### Common Integration Models in AP

| Model | Species | Ratio | Benefit | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vannamei + Tilapia (monosex) | 30 shrimp/m² + 0.5 tilapia/m² | — | Tilapia consume algae + waste | Tilapia breed; monosex only |
| Vannamei + Milkfish | 30 shrimp/m² + 0.2 milkfish/m² | — | Milkfish graze algae; good income | Less common in AP |
| Tiger + Mullet | 10 tiger/m² + 0.2 mullet/m² | — | Traditional AP system; mullet clean bottom | Requires management |
| Freshwater prawn + Rohu/Catla | 5 prawn/m² + 2 fish/m² | — | Traditional inland AP system | Water quality management |

### Rules for Shrimp-Fish Integration
1. Use only MONOSEX male tilapia — never mixed sex (will breed and crowd shrimp)
2. Do NOT use chemicals harmful to fish (e.g., malathion, dipterex) in integrated ponds
3. Do NOT use fish medications that affect shrimp (e.g., formalin at high dose)
4. Harvest fish before shrimp harvest — nets damage shrimp
5. Monitor DO more carefully — fish + shrimp = higher oxygen demand
6. Feed fish and shrimp separately if possible
