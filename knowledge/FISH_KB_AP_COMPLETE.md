## DISSOLVED OXYGEN — DETAILED GUIDE (ALL FISH)

### When DO Drops — Time-Based Risk
| Time | DO Status | Risk Level | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 AM – 5 AM | Lowest point of day | HIGH | Pre-set aerators to run all night |
| 5 AM – 7 AM | Rising slowly | MODERATE | Check fish behavior; be alert |
| 10 AM – 4 PM | Highest (photosynthesis peak) | LOW | Can reduce aerators if sufficient |
| After rain/overcast | Falls sharply | HIGH | Run aerators immediately |
| After algae crash | Critical crash | EMERGENCY | All aerators + water exchange |

### DO Emergency Response (All Fish)
1. Run ALL aerators at maximum speed immediately
2. STOP FEEDING completely
3. Open water inlet — bring fresh oxygenated water
4. Remove dead fish immediately (decomposition consumes more DO)
5. Do NOT add lime or probiotics during DO crash
6. After stabilization: find cause — overfeeding, algae crash, weather?

### AP-Specific DO Warnings
- **Summer (April–June):** Night DO crash risk is highest — run aerators 24/7
- **After heavy rain:** Sudden drop in DO due to surface stratification
- **Dense algae bloom:** Daytime DO can be very high (>12) but night crash risk is severe
- **Overcrowded ponds:** Tilapia at >5 fish/m² or Pangasius at >60/m² — aeration must be continuous

## pH MANAGEMENT — AP FISH PONDS

| Situation | pH Reading | Cause | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early morning low pH | 6.5–7.0 | CO₂ from night respiration | Normal if afternoon rises to 8.0+ |
| Afternoon high pH | 8.5–9.5 | Dense algae bloom | Water exchange 20%; reduce fertilizer |
| pH crash after rain | <6.5 | Acidic rain dilution | Lime 50–100 kg/acre immediately |
| Consistently low pH | <6.5 all day | Acidic soil; peat; low alkalinity | Agricultural lime 100–200 kg/acre; add dolomite |
| Dangerously high pH | >9.5 | Extreme algae bloom | Emergency water exchange + shade netting |

### pH Correction Products (AP)
| Problem | Product | Dose | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low pH (<6.5) | Agricultural lime (CaCO₃) | 100–200 kg/acre | Locally available everywhere in AP |
| Low pH (severe) | Quicklime (CaO) — empty pond only | 200–500 kg/acre | Use only between crops |
| Low alkalinity buffer | Dolomite | 50–100 kg/acre | Stabilizes pH swings |
| Low alkalinity buffer | Sodium bicarbonate | 5–10 kg/acre | Quick action for alkalinity boost |
| High pH (>9.5) | Water exchange (30–40%) | — | Dilution is fastest fix |

## AMMONIA — PRACTICAL GUIDE FOR AP FISH FARMERS

### What Causes High Ammonia in AP Ponds?
1. **Overfeeding** — leftover feed decomposes
2. **High stocking density** — fish excretion
3. **Dead fish not removed** — rotting releases ammonia rapidly
4. **Hot weather** — bacterial activity increases at >30°C
5. **Poor aeration** — low DO slows nitrification
6. **After pond fertilization** — organic fertilizer breakdown
7. **Post-rain** — flushing of accumulated organic matter

### Ammonia Control Protocol
| Step | Action | Product/Tool |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reduce/stop feeding | — |
| 2 | Run aerators at maximum | — |
| 3 | Apply zeolite | 50–100 kg/acre; morning application |
| 4 | 20–30% water exchange | Fresh, clean water only |
| 5 | Apply nitrifying probiotics | Biostadt Terragard-SP or Growel Pond Pro |
| 6 | Check and retest | Wait 24h; test again |

### Ammonia Toxicity by pH and Temperature
| Temp / pH | pH 7.0 | pH 7.5 | pH 8.0 | pH 8.5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 20°C | 0.4% toxic NH₃ | 1.2% | 3.8% | 11% |
| 25°C | 0.6% toxic NH₃ | 1.8% | 5.6% | 15.7% |
| 30°C | 1.0% toxic NH₃ | 3.0% | 8.8% | 23% |
**Note:** At pH 8.5 and 30°C (common AP summer conditions), nearly 23% of TAN is toxic — extremely dangerous!

## WATER COLOR GUIDE — AP FISH PONDS

| Water Color | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Light green (pea green) | Healthy Chlorella/diatom bloom | IDEAL — maintain |
| Brown-green | Mixed diatoms + detritus | GOOD for IMC polyculture |
| Dark green (thick) | Dense algae — crash risk | Reduce fertilizer; 20% water exchange |
| Blue-green (Cyanobacteria) | Toxic algae (Microcystis, Anabaena) | DANGEROUS — partial exchange + probiotics immediately |
| Reddish-brown | Dinoflagellates or iron-rich water | Investigate; water exchange |
| Clear/transparent | No algae — unproductive | Fertilize; urea 10 kg/acre + SSP 5 kg/acre |
| Black/gray | Anaerobic bottom release; H₂S | Aerate pond bottom urgently; lime application |
| Muddy/brown turbid | Physical turbidity (clay) | Alum 2–5 kg/acre; reduce water disturbance |
| Foamy/frothy surface | Organic overload or algae die-off | Reduce feeding; aerate; partial water exchange |

### Feed Requirements — GIFT Tilapia
| Stage | Protein % | Pellet Size | Key Nutrients |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fry (<1g) | 40–45% | Powder/0.3mm | High protein + DHA |

**Feed brands in AP:** CP Fish Feed, Growel fish feed, Godrej Real Fish Feed, Skretting Micro (for fry)
**Key feeding rule:** Tilapia is omnivore — can utilize plant protein (soybean, rice bran) well.

### GIFT Tilapia — Special Challenges
1. **Uncontrolled breeding (CRITICAL):**
   - Tilapia breed at 3–4 months old — suddenly pond has thousands of fry
   - Fry compete for feed → stunted growth → poor FCR → economic loss
   - **SOLUTION: Use all-male monosex fingerlings** (sex-reversed using 17α-methyltestosterone during fry stage at hatchery)
   - Ask hatchery specifically for "monosex GIFT tilapia" or "all-male tilapia"
   - Alternatively: manual sex separation (time-consuming for large farms)
   - Or: use Nile tilapia × Blue tilapia hybrid (naturally all-male in some crosses)

2. **Cold sensitivity:**
   - Below 15°C: immune system shuts down → disease outbreaks
   - AP winter (December–February): night temps can drop to 15–18°C in some zones
   - Action: Reduce feeding, avoid stocking in November–December in cooler AP zones

3. **Streptococcosis (Bacterial — biggest disease threat in AP):**
   - Emerges when temperature >28°C + high density + poor water quality
   - AP summer: high-density tilapia farms are very vulnerable

| pH | Daily | Morning + afternoon |
| Temperature | Daily | Morning |

## 2.2 INDIAN MAJOR CARPS (IMC) — POLYCULTURE
## Rohu (రోహు), Catla (కట్ల), Mrigal (మృగాళ)
## ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### Why Polyculture? The 3-Level Feeding Niche System
IMC polyculture is the most space-efficient freshwater fish farming system in India. Each species occupies a different vertical zone:

| Species | Local Name | Feeding Zone | Natural Food | Stocking % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Catla (Catla catla) | కట్ల | Surface | Zooplankton (copepods, cladocerans) | 30–40% |
| Rohu (Labeo rohita) | రోహు | Mid-water (column) | Phytoplankton, detritus, plant matter | 30–40% |
| Mrigal (Cirrhinus mrigala) | మృగాళ | Bottom | Detritus, organic sediment | 20–30% |
| Silver carp (optional) | — | Surface-column | Phytoplankton filter feeder | 5–10% |
| Grass carp (optional) | — | Column | Aquatic macrophytes | 5–10% |

**Result:** All 3 zones utilized → higher total yield per acre with less feed waste

### Seabass — Special Management Notes
1. **Cannibalism risk:** Seabass is highly cannibalistic, especially when size variation is high.
   - Sort by size every 4–6 weeks — keep uniform batches
   - Feed frequently (3x/day for small fish) to reduce cannibalism
   - Never miss a feeding — hungry seabass eat smaller fish

2. **Carnivore feed requirement:** Feed must have 35–50% protein. Low protein feed = poor growth + disease.

3. **Water quality sensitivity:** More sensitive to poor water quality than Tilapia or Pangasius.
   - Maintain DO >5 mg/L always
   - pH 7.0–8.5 strictly
   - Ammonia <0.5 mg/L

4. **Seed sourcing:** CMFRI, MPEDA-approved hatcheries, or private seabass hatcheries in AP coast

## 2.5 MURREL / SNAKEHEAD
## Local AP name: కొర్రమేను (Korra Meenu), Murrel
## Scientific: Channa striata / Channa marulius
## ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### About Murrel in AP
Murrel is one of AP's most valuable freshwater fish:
- Highest market price among freshwater fish: ₹300–600/kg
- Medicinal value — used post-surgery; high in PUFA
- Air-breathing fish: can survive in very low DO
- Carnivore: requires live/fresh fish feed or high-protein pellets
- Can be farmed in small ponds, tanks, and backyard systems
- **Channa striata** (Stripped murrel) = most farmed in AP
- **Channa marulius** (Great snakehead) = larger; less farmed

### Key Biological Parameters — Murrel
| Parameter | Channa striata (Korra Meenu) | Channa marulius |
|---|---|---|
| Optimal temperature | 25–32°C | 24–30°C |
| DO tolerance | Very low (air breather — can survive <1 mg/L) | Similar |

### Feed — Murrel Special Notes
| Feed Type | Effectiveness | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Live trash fish | BEST growth | Low FCR but disease risk from wild fish |
| Frozen trash fish | Good | Cost-effective; sterilize first |
| Moist pelleted feed (trash fish + binder) | Good | Mix 70% trash fish + 30% pellet |
| Formulated high-protein pellet (45%+) | Good if trained early | Must train from juvenile stage |
| Rice bran / low protein feeds | POOR | Murrel is carnivore — will not thrive |

**Pellet training protocol:**
- Start training from 10–20g stage
- Mix live/fresh fish with pellet gradually: week 1 = 80% fresh + 20% pellet; week 2 = 60/40; week 3 = 40/60; week 4 = fully on pellet
- Murrel trained on pellets from juvenile stage grows almost as well as fish-fed

### Murrel — Special Management
1. **Cannibalism:** Very high — size sort every 4–6 weeks is ESSENTIAL
2. **Air-breathing:** Must have air access above water — do not cover tanks fully
3. **Jumping:** Murrel jumps — net cover required on ponds/tanks
4. **Territorial:** Aggressive; at high density provide hiding structures (pipes, plants)
5. **Water quality:** Despite air-breathing tolerance, better water quality = better growth

## 3.1 STREPTOCOCCOSIS — #1 TILAPIA DISEASE IN AP

**Pathogen:** Streptococcus agalactiae, S. iniae, Lactococcus garvieae
**Species:** GIFT Tilapia (primary), Seabass, Salmon
**Mortality:** 30–50% if untreated; can devastate entire pond
**Risk season AP:** Summer (April–July) when temp >28°C + high stocking density
**Spread:** Direct fish-to-fish; water; through wounds

### Symptoms
- **Exophthalmia (Popeye):** One or both eyes bulging out — CLASSIC SIGN
- Hemorrhage (redness) around eyes and operculum (gill cover)
- Erratic/spiral/corkscrew swimming — nervous system damage
- Dark body pigmentation
- Fin hemorrhage
- Abnormal body posture (curved/arched body)
- Fish found dead near surface or banks
- Swollen, hemorrhagic spleen and kidney (internal)

### Diagnosis
- Clinical signs: popeye + spiral swimming in tilapia
- Lab: Gram-positive cocci in chains; beta-hemolytic on blood agar
- PCR test available at FISH/RGCA labs in AP

### Risk Factors in AP
- Temperature >28°C (very common AP summer)
- Stocking density >5 fish/m² without adequate aeration
- Poor water quality — low DO, high ammonia
- Stress from handling or crowding
- Introduction of infected fingerlings without quarantine

### Treatment Protocol
| Step | Action | Product | Dose | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reduce stocking density | — | Emergency harvest some fish if overcrowded | Immediate |
| 2 | Improve water quality | Aerators at max | — | Immediate |
| 3 | Antibiotic (vet prescription required) | Florfenicol (Virbac Florvet Aqua) | 10–15 mg/kg BW/day | 10–14 days |
| 3 (alt) | Antibiotic alternative | Amoxicillin (Intas Aquamox) | 80 mg/kg BW/day | 10 days |
| 4 | Immune support | Vitamin C | 2–3 g/kg feed | During + after treatment |
| 5 | Pond disinfection | Potassium permanganate | 2–3 ppm pond | After mortality stops |

### Prevention
- Use all-male monosex GIFT tilapia (less crowding from uncontrolled breeding)
- Maintain DO >5 mg/L especially in summer
- Quarantine all new fingerlings 14–21 days
- Vaccination: Tilapia Streptococcosis vaccine available commercially in India
- Monitor and maintain stocking density

## 3.2 TILAPIA LAKE VIRUS (TiLV) — EMERGING THREAT

**Pathogen:** Tilapia Lake Virus (novel orthomyxo-like RNA virus)
**Species:** GIFT Tilapia and all O. niloticus strains
**Mortality:** 20–90% — major threat to AP tilapia farms
**Status:** Spreading globally; confirmed in India

### Symptoms
- Exophthalmia (popeye — similar to Streptococcosis)
- Skin erosion, ulcers, scale loss
- Pale or hemorrhagic spleen
- Neurological signs: erratic swimming, spiral movement
- Lethargy; fish at pond surface
- High mortality especially in juveniles (<100g)
- **Differentiation from Strep:** Lab PCR needed; clinically similar

### Treatment
- NO antiviral treatment available
- Supportive care only
- Reduce stocking density
- Improve water quality
- Avoid stressing fish
- Notify fisheries department (notifiable disease)

### Prevention
- Source fingerlings from certified TiLV-free hatcheries
- Quarantine 21 days before stocking
- Biosecurity: disinfect equipment; limit movement between ponds

## 3.3 MOTILE AEROMONAS SEPTICEMIA (MAS) — FISH ULCER DISEASE

**Pathogen:** Aeromonas hydrophila, A. sobria
**Species:** All AP freshwater fish — IMC, Tilapia, Pangasius, Murrel
**Mortality:** 30–80% if untreated
**Risk season AP:** Post-monsoon to summer; stress events

### Symptoms
- **Hemorrhagic (bloody) skin lesions** — earliest sign
- **Ulcers** — punched-out circular wounds on body
- **Exophthalmia** (bulging eye)
- **Ascites** — swollen belly with fluid
- Scale lifting / scale loss
- Red inflamed anus
- Fin rot / tail rot
- **Internal:** hemorrhagic liver, spleen, kidney

### Diagnosis
- Clinical signs + bacterial culture
- Gram-negative short rods; oxidase positive
- TSA or blood agar: cream/yellow smooth colonies

### Treatment Protocol
| Priority | Treatment | Product | Dose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st choice | Antibiotic in feed | Florfenicol | 10–15 mg/kg BW × 10–14 days |
| 2nd choice | Antibiotic alternative | Oxytetracycline | 50–75 mg/kg BW × 14 days |
| External lesions | KMnO₄ bath | Potassium permanganate | 10–20 ppm × 30 min |
| External lesions | Iodine dip | Povidone iodine | 25–50 ppm × 15–20 min |
| Immune support | Vitamin C | Ascorbic acid | 2–3 g/kg feed × 14 days |
| Water quality | Probiotic restoration | Growel Pond Pro / Biostadt Optibact | 500g–1kg/acre |

### Prevention
- Maintain DO >5 mg/L
- Avoid physical injury during handling
- Vitamin C supplementation: 500 mg/kg feed routinely
- Probiotics in feed and water
- Reduce stocking density during stress seasons

## 3.4 EPIZOOTIC ULCERATIVE SYNDROME (EUS) — IMC KILLER

**Pathogen:** Aphanomyces invadans (Oomycete fungus — not a true fungus)
**Species:** Rohu (most susceptible), Catla, Mrigal, Murrel, Tilapia
**Mortality:** Can be 50–100% in rohu without treatment
**Season AP:** September–February (post-monsoon cooling period)
**Notifiable disease:** Must report to fisheries department

### Symptoms
- **Large red/brown ulcerative lesions** — deep, spreading wound
- Lesions often start near dorsal fin; spread to deep muscle
- Exposed bone in severe cases
- Hemorrhage around lesion edges
- Fish behave abnormally — surface swimming, lethargic
- Mottled skin color

### Diagnosis
- Clinical appearance of deep ulcers during cool season
- Lab: KOH preparation of lesion margin — hyphae of A. invadans visible
- PCR confirmation

### Treatment
| Treatment | Product | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Antifungal bath | Formalin | 25 ppm × 1 hour | Repeat weekly |
| Antifungal bath | KMnO₄ | 10 ppm × 30 min | |
| Antifungal bath | Salt (NaCl) | 5–10 g/L × 30 min | Cheap; effective |
| Lime pond treatment | Agricultural lime | 50–100 kg/acre | Raises pH; hostile to A. invadans |
| Antibiotic (secondary bacteria) | Oxytetracycline | 50 mg/kg × 14 days | Treats secondary bacterial infection |
| Topical on captured fish | Povidone iodine | Swab on lesion | Practical for cage culture |

### Prevention
- Lime application before post-monsoon season (September): 50 kg/acre
- Avoid introducing infected fish
- Maintain alkaline pH (>7.5) — A. invadans struggles at higher pH
- Good biosecurity — remove dead fish immediately
- Vitamin C in feed throughout cool season

## 3.5 COLUMNARIS DISEASE — SADDLEBACK / COTTON WOOL (FISH)

**Pathogen:** Flavobacterium columnare
**Species:** Pangasius (most severe in AP), Catfish, Tilapia, IMC
**Mortality:** Very high in Pangasius (50–100% if untreated); high in catfish

## 3.7 ICH (WHITE SPOT — FISH VERSION)

**Pathogen:** Ichthyophthirius multifiliis
**Species:** IMC (Rohu, Catla), Tilapia fingerlings, Catfish, Murrel
**Risk:** Cold water (<22°C), stress, poor nutrition, new fish introduction
**Mortality:** Very high in fry/fingerlings; moderate in adults

### Symptoms
- White salt-grain sized spots (1mm) on fins, body, gills — CLASSIC
- Fish rubbing against walls/bottom (flashing)
- Excess mucus (milky skin appearance)
- Gasping at surface (gill involvement)
- Clamped fins
- Loss of appetite

### Life Cycle (Critical for Treatment Timing)
1. Trophont (feeds inside fish skin): **NOT treatable**
2. Tomont (cyst on pond bottom): **Resistant to treatment**
3. Theront (free-swimming): **THIS IS THE TREATABLE STAGE**
- Full cycle: 3–7 days at 26°C; up to 30 days at 15°C

### Treatment
| Product | Dose | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Formalin | 25–50 ppm | Bath × 1 hour; repeat every 48h × 3 cycles | Reduces DO; aerate well |
| Salt (NaCl) | 3–5 g/L | Continuous pond application × 5–7 days | Safe; effective |
| KMnO₄ | 10–20 ppm | Bath × 30 min | |
| Copper sulfate | 0.25–0.5 ppm | Pond (adjust for pH) | Toxic to invertebrates; check pH first |
| Increase temperature | Raise to >28°C | — | Speeds up life cycle → faster kill |

# SECTION 5: POND MANAGEMENT — AP FISH
# ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

## POND PREPARATION PROTOCOL — AP FRESHWATER FISH PONDS

### Step 1: Draining and Drying (7–14 days)
- Drain pond completely
- Remove sludge / black soil from bottom (major disease reservoir)
- Dry until pond bottom cracks (2–3 cm deep cracks = good drying)
- **Tilling/plowing:** Plow 20–30 cm deep — oxygenate anaerobic layers
- AP tip: Summer (April–June) is the best pond preparation season due to intense sun

### Step 2: Liming
| Situation | Lime Type | Dose | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal pond | Agricultural lime (CaCO₃) | 200–300 kg/acre | pH adjustment + disinfection |
| Disease history pond | Quicklime (CaO) | 150–250 kg/acre | Strong disinfection |
| Acidic soil (pH <6.0) | Agricultural lime | 400–600 kg/acre | Soil pH correction |
| EUS history | CaO + CaCO₃ mix | 300 kg/acre total | Kill Aphanomyces |
| After liming | Let dry 1 week before water filling | — | — |

### Step 3: Water Filling
- Fill through fine mesh net (300 micron) to exclude wild fish and invertebrates
- Initial fill 50–60 cm depth; fill fully over 3–5 days
- Treat inlet water with chlorine (20 ppm) if disease risk is high; wait 3–5 days before stocking

### Step 4: Plankton Development (5–10 days)
| Fertilizer | Dose | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Cow dung (bagged in gunny sack) | 500–1000 kg/acre | Zooplankton and phytoplankton base |
| Urea | 10 kg/acre | Nitrogen for phytoplankton |
| Single Super Phosphate | 8 kg/acre | Phosphorus for plankton |
| Poultry manure | 200 kg/acre | Faster bloom development |

**Target before stocking:** Secchi depth 25–35 cm; green/brown-green water color; plankton bloom visible

### Step 5: Stocking
- Acclimatize fingerlings: float bags in pond for 20–30 minutes; add pond water gradually
- Stock in early morning or evening — avoid midday heat
- Add Vitamin C to pond water at stocking: 250g/acre — stress reduction
- Stock only healthy, uniform fingerlings

## ANTIBIOTIC USE — AP FISH FARMS
⚠️ All antibiotics require veterinary prescription.
⚠️ Observe withdrawal periods — do not harvest before withdrawal complete.
⚠️ Antibiotic use in aquaculture is regulated. Record all use.

| Disease | Antibiotic of Choice | Dose | Duration | Withdrawal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Streptococcosis (Tilapia) | Florfenicol | 10–15 mg/kg BW | 10–14 days | 15 days |
| Aeromonas (MAS) | Florfenicol | 10–15 mg/kg BW | 10–14 days | 15 days |
| Edwardsiellosis (Pangasius) | Florfenicol | 10–15 mg/kg BW | 10–14 days | 15 days |
| Columnaris | Oxytetracycline | 50–75 mg/kg BW | 10 days | 21 days |
| EUS (secondary bacteria) | Oxytetracycline | 50 mg/kg BW | 14 days | 21 days |
| Mixed bacterial | Amoxicillin | 80 mg/kg BW | 10 days | 14 days |

## ANDHRA PRADESH — MONTH-BY-MONTH FISH FARMING GUIDE

| Month | Season | Temperature | Key Risk | Key Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| January | Winter | 18–26°C | Cold stress; Ich; EUS; Saprolegnia | Reduce feed; monitor for white spots; lime ponds |
| February | Late winter | 20–28°C | EUS peak; Columnaris emerging | Formalin bath preventive; monitor IMC |
| March | Pre-summer | 25–32°C | Streptococcosis begins; Aeromonas | Watch tilapia for popeye; start Vit C in feed |
| April | Summer | 30–38°C | Streptococcosis peak; low DO nights; Vibrio | Run aerators all night; reduce density if needed |
| May | Peak summer | 32–40°C | Mass mortality risk (DO + disease + heat) | Maximum aeration; reduce feed; strict monitoring |
| June | Pre-monsoon | 30–36°C | Ammonia spike; algae instability | Probiotics; zeolite; reduce feed |
| July | Monsoon | 26–32°C | pH crash after rain; EUS starts early | Lime after rain; monitor pH daily |
| August | Monsoon | 26–32°C | Aeromonas post-rain; EUS risk | Vitamin C feed; biosecurity check |
| September | Post-monsoon | 26–32°C | EUS peak (cooler water coming); Anchor worm | Lime pond margins; KMnO₄ bath protocol |
| October | Post-monsoon | 24–30°C | EUS continuing; best harvest season starts | Plan harvest; check fish weight |
| November | Early winter | 22–28°C | Good growth; EUS winding down; Ich starts | Harvest larger fish; maintain feed |
| December | Winter | 18–26°C | Ich; Saprolegnia; cold stress; low feeding | Reduce feed; watch for white spots; saprolegnia |

## TILAPIA EMERGENCY DIAGNOSTIC

```
TILAPIA PROBLEM → START HERE

Is there MASS MORTALITY (>10 fish/day)?
├── YES:
│   ├── Check DO (5 AM reading):
│   │   ├── DO <3 mg/L → OXYGEN EMERGENCY → Run all aerators, stop feed
│   │   └── DO >4 mg/L → Disease suspected → Continue below
│   ├── Look at eyes: 
│   │   ├── BULGING EYE (Popeye)? → STREPTOCOCCOSIS → Florfenicol; vet required
│   │   ├── Normal eyes + ulcers? → AEROMONAS → Florfenicol; KMnO₄ bath
│   │   └── Normal eyes + white spots? → CHECK SALT GRAIN SPOTS on skin
│   │       ├── White spots (1mm) all over body → ICH → Salt treatment + Formalin bath
│   │       └── White cotton patches → COLUMNARIS or SAPROLEGNIA
│   └── TEMPERATURE: Is it >28°C + crowded pond? → HIGH Streptococcosis risk
└── NO (few deaths, slow decline):
    ├── Reduced feeding + slow growth → Check water quality; check for TiLV
    ├── Visible skin lesions/ulcers → AEROMONAS → KMnO₄ bath + consider antibiotic
    └── No obvious signs → Feed tray check; DO check; parasites exam
```

## PANGASIUS (JALIDI) EMERGENCY DIAGNOSTIC

```
PANGASIUS PROBLEM → START HERE

SPIRAL/CORKSCREW SWIMMING?
└── YES → EDWARDSIELLOSIS (Brain disease) → Florfenicol immediately (vet required)

SADDLEBACK WHITE-GRAY LESION ON DORSAL AREA?
└── YES → COLUMNARIS → KMnO₄ pond treatment; Florfenicol if severe

SWOLLEN BELLY (ASCITES)?
└── YES → AEROMONAS → Florfenicol; improve water quality; aeration

GASPING AT SURFACE (ALL FISH)?
└── YES → CHECK DO IMMEDIATELY
    ├── DO <2 mg/L → OXYGEN CRASH → All aerators maximum; stop feed; water exchange
    └── DO >3 mg/L → CHECK GILLS → Pale/necrotic gills → COLUMNARIS GILL ROT

HIGH FCR + NO DISEASE SIGNS?
└── CHECK: overfeeding? wrong feed? water quality? disease?
    → Feed tray check; water quality test; sampling for ABW
```

## IMC POLYCULTURE EMERGENCY DIAGNOSTIC

```
IMC PROBLEM → START HERE

ROHU SHOWING LARGE RED/BROWN ULCERS?
└── YES → EUS (Epizootic Ulcerative Syndrome)
    ├── Month: September–February? → HIGH PROBABILITY EUS
    └── Treatment: Formalin bath; lime pond; oxytetracycline

FISH JUMPING + RUBBING?
└── YES → PARASITES → Examine skin/gills under microscope
    ├── Visible worms on skin → ANCHOR WORM → KMnO₄ bath; manual removal
    ├── Disc-shaped lice on skin → FISH LICE (Argulus) → Salt bath; diflubenzuron
    └── Microscopic: look for flukes on gills → DACTYLOGYRUS → Formalin bath

VISIBLE WHITE SPOTS (1mm GRAIN-SIZED)?
└── YES → ICH → Salt (3–5 g/L continuous) + Formalin bath every 48h × 3

SURFACE GASPING ALL FISH?
└── DO CRASH → All aerators; stop feed; water exchange
```

## SEABASS (PANDUGAPPA) EMERGENCY DIAGNOSTIC

```
SEABASS PROBLEM → START HERE

SMALL FISH DISAPPEARING?
└── YES → CANNIBALISM → Sort by size immediately; increase feeding frequency

POPEYE + DARK PIGMENTATION?
└── YES → STREPTOCOCCOSIS or VIRAL NERVOUS NECROSIS (VNN)
    ├── Multiple fish affected → STREPTOCOCCOSIS → Florfenicol (vet)
    └── Nervous signs + larvae affected → VNN → No treatment; biosecurity

SKIN LESIONS / ULCERS?
└── YES → AEROMONAS or TENACIBACULUM
    └── Florfenicol; KMnO₄ bath; water quality improvement

GASPING AT SURFACE?
└── DO CHECK → Seabass needs >4 mg/L minimum
    └── <4 mg/L → OXYGEN EMERGENCY → All aerators + water exchange
```

## MURREL (KORRA MEENU) EMERGENCY DIAGNOSTIC

```
MURREL PROBLEM → START HERE

FISH JUMPING OUT OF POND?
└── LOW DO or STRESS → Check DO; check for disturbance/chemical addition
    → Cover pond/tank with net to prevent escape

SMALL FISH DISAPPEARING?
└── CANNIBALISM → Sort by size immediately; size segregation

RED LESIONS/ULCERS?
└── AEROMONAS → Florfenicol; KMnO₄ bath

NOT EATING FOR >3 DAYS?
└── Check temp (<22°C or >34°C = stop feeding naturally)
    └── If temp OK → disease/stress → examine fish; water quality check
```
